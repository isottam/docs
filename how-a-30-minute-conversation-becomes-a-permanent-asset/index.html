The system, end to end

How a 30-minute conversation becomes a permanent asset

You asked three things: how I file, how I tag so it stays findable, and the end part — turning a raw recording into something I can pull up months later. Here is the whole machine, one stage at a time.

Written for Isabella · the real pipeline, nothing hand-wavy

The trick is this: I don't file into folders. I file into metadata — and then a saved query does the cross-referencing for me, forever.

Folders force one note to live in one place. A conversation that touches three projects can only sit in one folder, so the tree fights you. Metadata and links don't have that problem — a note can belong to everything at once.

Stage 1 · Capture
01

The recording, with no thinking attached

A conversation gets recorded and dropped in automatically — no decisions made at this stage on purpose. The whole point is that capture is cheap and never asks me to categorise anything in the moment. Filing decisions are the thing that kills note-taking; so capture makes zero of them.

What actually happens

An iOS recording lands as a file, untouched. It goes into one flat folder — 15. Transcripts/ — alongside ~400 others. No sub-folders by person, project or month.

Why it's "dumb"

It carries one type-tag, #MeetingSummary, and nothing else yet. The intelligence comes later, in one deliberate pass. Capture stays brainless so it never becomes a chore.

In plain termsGet the raw thing in the door with zero friction. Don't decide where it "belongs" yet — that decision is what makes people stop taking notes.
Stage 2 · Transcribe
02

Speech becomes text, with voices kept apart

The audio is auto-transcribed and diarised — a fancy word for "the speakers are separated". The transcriber doesn't know who is who; it just knows there were two voices and labels them spk_0 and spk_1. Naming them correctly is a later job, and it's the one I'm most careful about.

The tool

AWS Transcribe sits behind the recording app. Out comes a transcript where every line is stamped with which voice said it — but only as spk_0 / spk_1.

The trap I avoid

Guessing who's who from "this one sounds more like the marketing person" is how you misattribute a quote. Misattribution is the single worst failure — so naming voices gets evidence, not vibes (Stage 4).

In plain termsThe machine writes down everything and tags each line with a voice number. It does not yet know your name from mine — and pretending it does is where most systems go wrong.
Stage 3 · Raw artefact
03

A real file, but still inert

Now there's a transcript sitting in 15. Transcripts/. It's a genuine record — but it's not yet findable or connected to anything. If I left it here, in six months I'd never locate it. This is exactly the stage you said you were stuck on: "I have the transcription pipeline, I'm just figuring out the end part." The end part is everything after this.

What it has

Verbatim dialogue, speaker numbers, a timestamp. The auto-summary at the top is shallow — it flattens the numbers, names and stories that actually matter.

What it's missing

No real author. No topics from my controlled list. No links to the people in it. No analysis. No connection to past conversations. Raw, not yet an asset.

In plain termsYou have the conversation written down. That is necessary but useless on its own — a box of facts with no index. The next single step is what turns it into something you can actually retrieve and trust.
Stage 4 · The turning point
04

One pass that does five things, in order

This is the whole game — a single processing pass run over the raw transcript. It's a defined routine (I call it /transcript) and it always does the same five things, in the same order. This is the "end part" you're missing. Each sub-step below is a real, named mechanism, not a vague "and then I tidy it up".

1 · Lock down who said what

Build a speaker map from dispositive quotes — lines that can only have been said by one person (e.g. someone asking the other about their INSEAD exams must be the non-student). Two pieces of hard evidence per voice. If I can't prove it, I stop and ask rather than guess.

2 · Load the relationship

Pull the People card and every prior note/transcript for everyone in the room before writing. So the new note already knows the history — last time we spoke, what's open, how warm the relationship is.

3 · Synthesise, don't transcribe

Write a fresh analytical note in 4. Notes/: key points framed as implications not recap, real quotes pulled from the raw text, action items, open questions. The raw transcript stays attached at the bottom, verbatim. The value is the thinking on top.

4 · Tag the raw file too

Backfill the original transcript's Topics, Author, Description so even the raw artefact becomes queryable — not just the polished note. Nothing is left un-indexed.

5 · Update the CRM

Bump the person's People card: last-interaction date, interaction count +1, relationship strength re-assessed (New → Familiar → Active), and a new row in their interaction log linking to this note. The conversation now lives in their file too, not just the meeting's.

In plain termsThis is the one habit that matters. Instead of saving a transcript and hoping, you run it through a fixed checklist that names the speakers properly, writes the real takeaways, tags everything from a fixed list, and updates the person's record. Five steps, same every time. Do this and the conversation stops being a recording and becomes a permanent, trustworthy asset.
Stage 5 · Fan-out
05

The note wires itself into everything related

Here's the payoff, and it's the bit that feels like magic but isn't. I never manually cross-link notes. Every note ends with a "Related" block that is a live query, not a hand-typed list. The moment a note is filed with good metadata, that query automatically surfaces it next to every other note that shares an author, a topic or a tag — scored and ranked.

The live query (Bases)

A saved query runs on every note, comparing its author/topics/tags against the whole vault and listing the closest matches. Add metadata once — the web of links builds itself.

The compounding effect

A conversation from six months ago resurfaces exactly when I open a related note — because they share a topic or a person. The People cards do the same for relationships. Nothing rots.

In plain termsYou don't keep a map of how everything connects. You tag each note well, and a saved search draws the map for you — and redraws it every time something new comes in. That's why old conversations pop back up at the right moment.
Your first two questions, answered

Flat filing, three-layer tagging

📁

  1. The filing (the "flat" part)

Almost everything lives in two flat folders. There is no "INSEAD › P2 › Finance › Lectures" tree. Everything sits at one level and is organised by metadata instead.

  • 4. Notes/ — the hub. Synthesis notes, write-ups, anything thought-through. New notes default here.
  • 5. Sources/ — external material: articles, podcasts, readings.
  • 15. Transcripts/ — raw recordings, ~400 of them, no sub-folders.
  • A few specialist folders exist (People, Wine, Recipes) for genuinely distinct object types — but never nested by topic.
  • One rule makes flatness work: every note carries Up: [[Map]] at the top — a link to one central map. The hierarchy is a link graph, not a folder tree. You navigate by following links and queries, never by clicking down through folders.
🏷️

  1. The tagging (how it stays findable)

Three layers, and the discipline is the whole trick. The fixed lists are what stop tags rotting into 50 near-duplicates.

  • Topics — from a fixed allowed list of ~25 (a controlled vocabulary: "AI and Technology", "Leadership and Startup", "INSEADCourse"…). Not free-form. A closed list means every note on a subject is tagged with the same word — so one query returns all of it.
  • Entity tags — #CamelCase inline tags for people, orgs and concepts (#Anthropic, #ClaudeCode). 3–6 per note. The fine-grained threads.
  • Type tags — one per note marking what kind of thing it is: #MeetingSummary, #task, #resource, #idea.
  • Author field — on top of the three, an Author field in CamelCase (FreddieChambers, IsabellaIsotta) ties every note to the people in it.
Why findability is automatic

The query that ranks "related" for me

The "Related" block at the foot of every note scores all other notes against this one and shows the top matches. I tune nothing by hand — I just tag well. The scoring is simple:

+3
Shared authorThe same person appears in both notes — the strongest signal that two notes belong together.
+2
Each shared tagEvery #CamelCase entity tag in common adds two points.
+1
Each shared topicEvery topic from the fixed list in common adds one point.
Notes are ranked by total score, highest first. Tag a note well once, and it wires itself into everything related — permanently, with no manual cross-linking.
